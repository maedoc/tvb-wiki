# MEG

Magnetoencephalography (MEG) is a non-invasive neuroimaging technique that measures magnetic fields produced by neural activity. It offers similar temporal resolution to EEG with improved spatial resolution.

## Definition

MEG detects magnetic fields perpendicular to electrical currents generated by neural activity. Unlike EEG, MEG signals are not distorted by the skull, providing better spatial localization of sources. MEG primarily captures activity from superficial cortical sources oriented parallel to the scalp surface.

## Characteristics

| Feature | Typical Value |
|---------|---------------|
| Temporal resolution | 1 ms |
| Spatial resolution | 2-5 mm (theoretical) |
| Frequency range | DC-600 Hz |
| Sensitivity | ~fT (femtotesla) |

## Comparison with EEG

| Aspect | MEG | EEG |
|--------|-----|-----|
| Signal | Magnetic fields | Electric potentials |
| Skull effect | Minimal attenuation | Strong blurring |
| Source sensitivity | Tangential sources | All orientations |
| Spatial resolution | Better (~5mm) | Poorer (~1cm) |
| Cost | Higher | Lower |
| Portability | Fixed systems | Portable systems |

## Role in Whole-Brain Modeling

MEG provides complementary validation targets to EEG:

1. **Better source localization**: Less volume conduction blur enables more precise spatial validation
2. **Frequency analysis**: Similar oscillatory bands as EEG (alpha, beta, gamma)
3. **Connectivity**: Phase relationships between regions

Like EEG, MEG signals can be generated from [[neural-mass-model]] outputs through forward models that account for the magnetic field patterns of current sources.

## Analysis Methods

- **Beamforming**: Spatial filtering for source localization
- **Minimum-norm estimation**: Distributed source reconstruction
- **Time-frequency analysis**: Similar methods as EEG
- **Connectivity**: Coherence, phase locking, Granger causality
