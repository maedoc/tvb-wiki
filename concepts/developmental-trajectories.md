# Developmental Trajectories

Developmental trajectories describe the patterns of change in brain structure and function across the lifespan, from prenatal development through aging. These trajectories are often non-linear, with different brain regions following distinct developmental curves.

## Definition

A developmental trajectory is the path of change in a brain measure over time, characterizing how structure, function, or connectivity evolve across age. Trajectories can be linear or non-linear, and vary across individuals and brain regions.

## Trajectory Patterns

### Linear Trajectories
- Constant rate of change
- Simple increase or decrease
- Rare in brain development

### Non-Linear Trajectories
- **Inverted U-shape**: Increase then decrease
  - Synaptic density (pruning)
  - Some cognitive functions
  
- **U-shape**: Decrease then increase
  - Less common in brain measures
  
- **Logarithmic**: Rapid early change, slowing
  - White matter development
  - Language acquisition
  
- **Sigmoidal**: Slow-fast-slow
  - Pubertal development
  - Some structural changes

## Regional Differences

### Cortical Development
Following Gogtay et al. (2004):

1. **Primary regions** (sensory/motor)
   - Earliest maturation
   - Peak in childhood
   - Gradual decline

2. **Association regions** (temporal/parietal)
   - Intermediate maturation
   - Peak in adolescence
   - Moderate decline

3. **Prefrontal cortex**
   - Latest maturation
   - Peak in early adulthood
   - Steeper decline

### White Matter Development
- **Early life**: Rapid myelination
- **Adolescence**: Continued maturation
- **Adulthood**: Slow decline
- **Aging**: Accelerated loss

## Network Trajectories

### Functional Connectivity
- **Childhood**: Local, short-range connections
- **Adolescence**: Shift to long-range
- **Adulthood**: Distributed networks
- **Aging**: Connectivity decline

### Network Topology
- **Modularity**: Increases then plateaus
- **Efficiency**: Improves then declines
- **Hub organization**: Strengthens then weakens

## Individual Differences

### Sources of Variation
- **Genetics**: Heritability of trajectories
- **Environment**: Experience-dependent changes
- **Sex differences**: Hormonal influences
- **Socioeconomic status**: Access to resources

### Sensitive Periods
- Windows of heightened plasticity
- Experience has maximal impact
- Different for different functions
- Language, sensory, social development

## Critical Periods

### Definition
- Time-limited windows
- Experience essential for development
- Irreversible if missed
- Examples:
  - Visual system (infancy)
  - Language (early childhood)
  - Social bonding (attachment)

## Role in Whole-Brain Modeling

### Age-Specific Models
- Different parameters for different ages
- Trajectory-informed constraints
- Developmentally appropriate dynamics

### Individual Prediction
- Forecast future states
- Identify atypical trajectories
- Early intervention targets

### Lifespan Models
- Continuous change functions
- Integration of development and aging
- U-shaped trajectories
